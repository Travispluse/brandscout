# Run a Founder Profile Brand QA Before Launch

New brands often launch through people before they launch through pages.

The homepage may be correct. The domain may redirect cleanly. The company LinkedIn page may have the right logo and category. Then a potential customer clicks the founder's profile and sees last year's project name, an old personal site, a half-written bio, a calendar link with the wrong company, and a headline that explains the business three different ways.

That is not a small detail.

For an early-stage company, founder profiles are part of the launch surface. They show up in announcement posts, comments, DMs, investor intros, podcast notes, hiring conversations, partner decks, Product Hunt profiles, GitHub organizations, newsletter bylines, and search results. People often trust the person before they trust the company.

A founder profile brand QA is a short review of the public profiles and personal routes that can teach people what the new brand is called, where it lives, what category it belongs in, and how to reach it. It is not personal brand coaching. It is not a vanity rewrite. It is a practical launch check for places where the founder's public identity can either reinforce or fragment the brand.

If the core launch facts are still moving, set the [brand freeze date](/blog/brand-freeze-date-before-launch) first. If the profile copy keeps changing because nobody agrees on what the product is, build the [category language sheet](/blog/category-language-sheet-before-launch). This article starts after the brand facts are chosen and before the founders start sending public attention toward them.

## List The Founder Surfaces That People Will Actually Click

Do not start with every account a founder has ever created.

Start with the places someone in your launch audience is likely to see during the first two weeks.

| Surface | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| LinkedIn profile | Often the first credibility check for B2B buyers, partners, and candidates |
| X, Threads, Bluesky, or other public social accounts | Announcement posts and replies can outrank the company profile early |
| GitHub profile | Important for developer tools, open source projects, and technical founders |
| Personal website | Can rank for the founder name and send people to the wrong project |
| Newsletter, Substack, Medium, or podcast profile | Carries bylines, bios, and audience trust |
| Product Hunt or launch community profile | Often links the founder directly to the launch page |
| Calendar page | Converts attention into meetings, so the branding needs to feel official |
| Email signature | Travels through intros, replies, investor notes, and customer conversations |
| Speaker, advisor, or portfolio bios | Old blurbs get copied into new launch contexts |

The goal is not to sanitize a founder's whole internet history. The goal is to clean the surfaces that will actively influence the new brand's first impression.

Make a small inventory with the profile URL, owner, public audience, current link, current description, and status. If there are three founders, do this for all three. A launch can look messy when each founder tells a slightly different version of the company.

## Start With The Approved Public Pattern

Before editing any profile, write the approved pattern in one place.

| Field | Approved answer |
| --- | --- |
| Public brand name | Northline |
| Canonical URL | `https://getnorthline.com` |
| Company profile | `linkedin.com/company/northline` |
| Primary handle | `@getnorthline` |
| Category phrase | Scheduling software for home service teams |
| Founder role language | Co-founder of Northline |
| Public contact route | `hello@getnorthline.com` or `/contact` |
| Demo route | `getnorthline.com/demo` |

This table keeps profile edits from becoming a copywriting free-for-all.

It should match the [canonical brand URL](/blog/canonical-brand-url-before-launch), the [social handle audit](/blog/social-handle-audit-before-launch), and the [brand contact route map](/blog/brand-contact-route-map-before-launch). If those documents disagree, fix the disagreement before touching profiles.

Founder profiles are not the place to test five versions of the positioning. They are distribution. Distribution should repeat the decision, not reopen it.

## Fix The First Two Lines People See

Most people will not read the full bio.

They will see a name, headline, avatar, banner image, profile link, and maybe one short intro line. That top area needs to carry the brand cleanly.

Check:

- Display name.
- Headline or title.
- Current company field.
- Profile photo.
- Banner or cover image.
- Short bio.
- Featured link.
- Pinned post.
- Creator tagline or newsletter description.

A weak profile top area sounds like this:

| Profile detail | Problem |
| --- | --- |
| `Founder, builder, advisor, AI tinkerer` | Does not connect the person to the launch |
| `Working on something new` | Wastes attention during announcement week |
| `northline-beta.vercel.app` | Teaches the wrong URL |
| `@northlinehq` in one bio and `@getnorthline` in another | Splits the handle pattern |
| Old banner from the previous project | Makes the launch look like a side note |

A stronger version is boring in the right way:

| Profile detail | Better launch version |
| --- | --- |
| Headline | `Co-founder of Northline, scheduling software for home service teams` |
| Bio line | `Building Northline to help HVAC, plumbing, and field teams keep schedules clear.` |
| Link | `https://getnorthline.com` |
| Pinned post | Launch post with the approved name, URL, category, and company profile tag |
| Email signature | `Maya Chen, Co-founder, Northline` with a company-domain email |

This is not about making founders sound corporate. It is about making the public trail easy to follow.

## Make Every Link Point To The Right Next Step

Profile links carry more weight than teams expect.

A founder may have one link slot on LinkedIn, X, GitHub, a newsletter profile, or a launch community page. During launch, that link should not point to a personal homepage unless the personal homepage has a clear route to the company. It should not point to an old waitlist if the public site is live. It should not point to a calendar page if the real next step is signup.

Use a simple rule: the profile link should match the action you want that audience to take.

| Profile audience | Better link |
| --- | --- |
| Customers | Homepage, product page, pricing page, or signup |
| Investors | Company homepage or concise investor-safe overview |
| Candidates | Careers, about page, or founder post with context |
| Developers | Docs, GitHub organization, or API page |
| Journalists | Press page or contact route |
| Partners | Partner page, demo route, or contact form |

If there is only one link slot, choose the highest-value route for launch week. Use the pinned post or profile text to carry secondary links.

This pairs with the [brand link preview QA](/blog/brand-link-preview-qa-before-launch). Founder profile links are pasted into messages, cards, previews, and screenshots. If the preview title, URL, or image is stale, the profile can spread the wrong version of the brand even when the link technically works.

## Retire Old Project Breadcrumbs

Founders often carry old projects in their bios because those projects are part of their story.

That can be fine. It becomes a launch problem when the old project competes with the new brand.

Look for:

- Old company names in headlines.
- Previous domains in profile links.
- Abandoned products in pinned posts.
- Old screenshots in featured sections.
- Newsletter descriptions that still mention the prior category.
- Speaker bios that position the founder around a different market.
- Personal sites where the new company is missing or buried.
- GitHub profile READMEs that point to an old organization first.

You do not need to erase the past. You do need to set hierarchy.

For launch week, the new brand should be the obvious current project. Old work can move to a "previously" line, an archive section, or a portfolio page that does not steal the primary link.

This matters for search too. A [branded search dry run](/blog/branded-search-dry-run-before-launch) should include founder-name searches. If a buyer searches the founder and the first results point to an unrelated old product, the brand has extra explaining to do.

## Align Calendar Pages And Personal Routes

Calendar links are small conversion pages.

They often lag behind the brand because they live in a separate tool. The founder updates the homepage but forgets the scheduling page. A prospect clicks "book time" and lands on a page with the old company name, a personal Gmail sender, a different avatar, and a meeting description written for the previous offer.

Check:

- Calendar page title.
- Booking URL or slug.
- Host name and avatar.
- Meeting type names.
- Confirmation email sender.
- Reminder email copy.
- Time zone and location.
- Routing questions.
- Thank-you page.
- Cancellation and reschedule messages.

If the calendar is public, make it feel like part of the brand. Use the company-domain email where possible. Rename the meeting type from `Intro with Maya` to something clearer like `Northline demo with Maya` if the route is for prospects. Remove stale questions. Make the confirmation email tell people what they booked and which brand will follow up.

If the calendar should not be public yet, remove it from founder profiles and use the official contact route instead.

The [brand contact route map](/blog/brand-contact-route-map-before-launch) is useful here because personal calendars can become accidental sales, support, press, and partnership routes. Decide whether that is intentional.

## Make Founder Tags Reinforce The Company Account

When founders post about launch, they often tag the company account.

That tag needs to work.

Before announcement day:

- Confirm the company profile exists and is visible.
- Confirm the founder can tag the right account.
- Check that the company account has the approved display name, avatar, URL, and bio.
- Make sure each founder uses the same tag pattern.
- Remove tags to placeholder, duplicate, or abandoned accounts.
- Add founders as admins where the platform requires it.

This is where the [brand asset handoff sheet](/blog/brand-asset-handoff-sheet-before-launch) becomes practical. If only one founder can access the LinkedIn company page, the launch is fragile. If a contractor created the profile and never handed it over, the founders may not be able to fix a mistake when attention arrives.

For small teams, one wrong tag can teach people the wrong account quickly. A company account with no avatar, no link, and no post history looks less official than the founder who tagged it.

## Keep Multiple Founders From Creating Multiple Brands

Multiple founders make the problem harder.

One founder says "AI scheduling for contractors." Another says "field service operations." A third says "workflow software for local teams." None of those lines may be terrible, but together they force outsiders to assemble the category themselves.

Run a side-by-side profile check:

| Field | Founder A | Founder B | Founder C | Approved |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Company name | Northline | NorthLine | Northline AI | Northline |
| Category phrase | Scheduling for contractors | Field ops software | AI workflow tool | Scheduling software for home service teams |
| URL | `getnorthline.com` | `northline.co` | `northline.app` | `getnorthline.com` |
| Company tag | Correct | Missing | Old profile | Correct |
| Contact route | Personal calendar | Website | Personal email | Website or approved demo route |

This table usually reveals the work fast.

Do not make every founder sound identical. Do make the public facts identical: name, capitalization, URL, category, handle, company tag, and contact route. Tone can vary. Facts should not.

## Test The Profile Path Like A Stranger

After edits, run the path from the outside.

Open a private browser window and search:

- Founder name.
- Founder name plus brand name.
- Brand name plus founder name.
- Founder handle.
- Founder name plus category.

Then click through the visible results.

Ask:

- Can a stranger tell what the founder is building now?
- Does the founder profile point to the official brand?
- Does the company profile look complete enough to trust?
- Do links land on the canonical URL?
- Do calendar and email routes feel official?
- Are old projects clearly secondary?
- Does mobile cropping hide or distort the important profile details?

This check catches issues that profile edit screens hide. Search snippets may keep old titles. Social cards may cache old descriptions. Mobile layouts may truncate the category phrase after the least useful words. A link may redirect through a personal domain before landing on the company site.

Fix what a real visitor would see, not just what the admin panel says.

## Keep A Tiny Founder Profile QA Record

Do not make this a giant brand governance document.

A useful record can be one table:

| Profile | Owner | Approved link | Approved category line | Last checked | Fix needed |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Maya LinkedIn | Maya | `getnorthline.com` | Scheduling software for home service teams | June 16 | Update banner |
| Maya X | Maya | Launch post | Same as approved | June 16 | Pin post |
| Dev GitHub | Engineering | `github.com/northline` | Developer docs link | June 16 | Add company org link |
| Founder calendar | Maya | `/demo` | Northline demo | June 16 | Change confirmation email |

Put the table near the launch QA work, not in a random personal notes file. It belongs beside the brand freeze, contact route map, and launch copy QA because the same public facts are being checked.

Review it again after launch week. Founder profiles change quickly during a launch because people are replying, reposting, updating links, joining podcasts, speaking in communities, and sending intros. A profile that was clean on Tuesday can drift by Friday.

## The Practical Standard

Founder profiles do not need to become polished corporate brochures.

They need to stop contradicting the brand.

Before launch, every visible founder surface should answer four questions clearly:

- What is the brand called?
- What does it do?
- Where should I go?
- Is this person officially connected to it?

If those answers are consistent, the founder's public presence becomes an asset. Searchers, customers, investors, candidates, and partners get the same trail from person to company to product.

If those answers are scattered, the founder becomes an accidental source of brand drift.

Use BrandScout to check the name, domain, and handle pattern before the launch push. Then make sure the founders who will carry that launch in public are pointing people to the same version of the brand.
